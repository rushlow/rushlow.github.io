Job Market Paper

Bioeconomic Implications of the Interaction of Middlemen and distance: The Case of a Bushmeat Supply Chain

Jennifer R. Rushlow

Abstract: Resource supply chains in low and middle income countries (LMIC) often depend on middlemen to transport the harvested resource to a local market for sale. These middlemen may drive the extraction locations and levels of the resource and therefore their decisions should be included in models of resource extraction. I model middlemen, both monopsonistic and competitive, as well as local hunters, and show that middlemen can induce harvesting in more distant locations than hunters alone, and middlemen may continue to harvest at lower stock levels. I find that monopsonistic middlemen protect resources at closer locations in a single period which is consistent with the literature. However, I also find that this result does not necessarily hold over time because these middlemen continue to harvest at lower stock levels. I apply these results to a case study of the fruit bat supply chain in Sulawesi, Indonesia to explain harvesting expansion and continued harvesting despite low population levels.

Disease Risk from Human–Environment Interactions: Environment and Development Economics for Joint Conservation‑Health Policy

Heidi J. Albers, Katherine D. Lee, Jennifer R. Rushlow, Carlos Zambrana-Torrelio

Environmental and Resource Economics,76(4), pp.929-944.

Abstract: Emergence of COVID-19 joins a collection of evidence that local and global health are influenced by human interactions with the natural environment. Frameworks that simultaneously model decisions to interact with natural systems and environmental mechanisms of zoonotic disease spread allow for identification of policy levers to mitigate disease risk and promote conservation. Here, we highlight opportunities to broaden existing conservation economics frameworks that represent human behavior to include disease transmission in order to inform conservation-disease risk policy. Using examples from wildlife markets and forest extraction, we call for environment, resource, and development economists to develop and analyze empirically-grounded models of people’s decisions about interacting with the environment, with particular attention to LMIC settings and ecological-epidemiological risk factors. Integrating the decisions that drive human–environment interactions with ecological and epidemiological research in an interdisciplinary approach to understanding pathogen transmission will inform policy needed to improve both conservation and disease spread outcomes.

Working Papers

How the removal of a market barrier enhanced market efficiency: the case of WTI and Brent crude oil prices

Paul Bauer, Jennifer Rushlow

Under Review

Abstract: If the world oil market is efficient, arbitrage should lead the West Texas Intermediate (WTI) and Brent crude oil price indexes to converge whenever they stray apart, or to be more statistically precise, they should be cointegrated with an error correction model (ECM) describing how quickly the prices converge following a shock. From the beginning of our sample on May 20, 1987 until December 1, 2010 when U.S. oil production began to accelerate due to hydraulic fracturing (fracking), we find the two series are cointegrated. However, from this point formerly nonbinding constraints became binding. The domestic and world markets were well adapted to the US being a large importer of oil, but the increased production from fracking disrupted this equilibrium, making the decades old U.S. ban on crude oil exports and pipeline capacity from the new well sites to the existing refineries binding. From late 2010 until the crude export ban was lifted on December 18, 2015, the two prices are not cointegrated. Since the lifting of the ban through to October 31, 2016, the end of our sample, the two prices are again cointegrated, indicating the transatlantic market for crude oil is operating more efficiently. We also find evidence the convergence in the two series is now occurring more quickly in this last period than in the first period, suggesting another source of efficiency gains.

Household choice of water storage and water source in rural Guatemala

Jennifer Rushlow and Tabaré Capitán

Abstract: In rural Guatemala households have unreliable tap water access and must choose how to cope. A household can either store water or use alternative sources and this choice has potential implications for the households health. We investigate which household characteristics increase the likelihood of a household using safer sources and storage types. We find that income and education have no significant impact on the likelihood of a household using most storage and source types. However, what a household uses water for has a large, significant, effect on the household’s choice of storage and source.

Assessing the optimal control of white-nose syndrome in bats with a limited budget and multiple control types

Jennifer Rushlow

Description: I introduce costs and budgetary limitations into an ecological model of bat migration and infection with multiple control types. I assess the optimal control techniques for white-nose syndrome and use a simulation to evaluate the best use of limited resources for treatment and prevention.

Yasha Feferholtz, Jennifer Rushlow, Carlos Zambrana-Torrelio, Peter Daszak
